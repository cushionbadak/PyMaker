<p>Creating two strings:</p>
<pre><code>s1 = "sha1:abcd"
s2 = "sha1:wxyz"
</code></pre>
<p>Applying <code>.strip()</code> function on both strings:</p>
<pre><code>s1.strip("sha1:")
&gt;&gt;&gt; 'bcd'

s2.strip("sha1:")
&gt;&gt;&gt; 'wxyz'
</code></pre>
<p>I expected the following output:</p>
<pre><code>s1.strip("sha1:")
&gt;&gt;&gt; 'abcd'

s2.strip("sha1:")
&gt;&gt;&gt; 'wxyz'
</code></pre>
<p>I am aware that <code>strip()</code> function is deprecated. I am just curious to know the issue. I went through official docs, but found no special mentions about ":a" or anything like that. </p>
<p>And also I am aware of other alternatives, we can use <code>split("sha1:")</code> or <code>strip("sha1")</code> followed by <code>strip(":")</code>, gives the desired output.</p>

<div class="post-text" itemprop="text">
<p>there</p>
<pre><code>strip(...)
    S.strip([chars]) -&gt; str

    Return a copy of the string S with leading and trailing
    whitespace removed.
    If chars is given and not None, remove characters in chars instead.
</code></pre>
<p>note <strong>characters in chars</strong></p>
<p>Explained in detail in the <a href="https://docs.python.org/3/library/stdtypes.html#str.strip" rel="nofollow noreferrer">documentation</a>.</p>
</div>
<div class="post-text" itemprop="text">
<p>Here is a counter example showing the <em>actual</em> intention of <code>strip</code>:</p>
<pre><code>s1 = "sha1:abcds"
s2 = "sha1:wxyzs"

print(s1.strip("sha1:"))
print(s2.strip("sha1:"))
</code></pre>
<p>Output:</p>
<pre><code>bcd
wxyz
</code></pre>
<p><code>strip()</code> removed chars supplied in its parameter, whether they are found at the start or end of the target.</p>
</div>
<div class="post-text" itemprop="text">
<p>It will strip all characters i.e. <code>s</code>, <code>h</code>, <code>a</code> , <code>1</code> and <code>:</code> at the beginning and ending of the string. </p>
