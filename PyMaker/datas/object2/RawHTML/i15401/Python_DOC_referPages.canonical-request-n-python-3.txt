<div class="post-text" itemprop="text">
<p>So you want to not have "actual" newlines, but the escape character for newlines in your string? Just add a second slash to <code>'\n'</code> to escape it as well, <code>'\\n'</code>. Or prepend your strings with r to make them "raw"; in them the backslash is interpreted literally; <code>r'\n'</code> (commonly used for regular expressions).</p>
<pre><code>canonical_request = method + r'\n' + canonical_uri + r'\n' + canonical_querystring + r'\n' + canonical_headers
</code></pre>
<p>For information about string literals, see <a href="https://docs.python.org/3/reference/lexical_analysis.html#string-and-bytes-literals" rel="nofollow">String and byte literals</a> in the docs.</p>
</div>
<div class="post-text" itemprop="text">
<p>As an alternative and more elegant way you can put your strings in a list and join them with escape the <code>\n</code> with add <code>\</code> to leading  : </p>
<pre><code>&gt;&gt;&gt; l=['method', 'canonical_uri', 'canonical_querystring', 'canonical_headers']
&gt;&gt;&gt; print '\\n'.join(l)
method\ncanonical_uri\ncanonical_querystring\ncanonical_headers
</code></pre>
<blockquote>
<p>The backslash (\) character is used to escape characters that otherwise have a special meaning, such as newline, backslash itself, or the quote character.</p>
</blockquote>
