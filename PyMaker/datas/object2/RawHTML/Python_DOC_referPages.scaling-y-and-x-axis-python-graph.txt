<div class="post-text" itemprop="text">
<p>You could use <code>plt.xlim([-10,0])</code> and <code>plt.ylim([-10,0])</code> to specify the minimum and maximum values of your axes.</p>
</div>
<div class="post-text" itemprop="text">
<p>I ran your code, and got the following chart:</p>
<p><a href="https://i.stack.imgur.com/flPMi.png" rel="nofollow noreferrer"><img alt="enter image description here" src="https://i.stack.imgur.com/flPMi.png"/></a></p>
<p>Is your concern that the data points along the lower end of your y axis are "scrunched up"? If that's the case, perhaps plotting on the log axis might help. Use the <code>set_yscale('log')</code> method like so:</p>
<pre><code>ax = plt.gca()
ax.set_yscale('log')
</code></pre>
<p>With that, I get the following chart:</p>
<p><a href="https://i.stack.imgur.com/vOmKo.png" rel="nofollow noreferrer"><img alt="enter image description here" src="https://i.stack.imgur.com/vOmKo.png"/></a></p>
<p>The issue with this of course is that some of the y-axis values are negative, and thus can't be directly plotted on a log scale. The complete solution would involve adding a constant to all currents such that they're positive.</p>
<p>PS -- I think there's a bug in one of your <code>plt.plot</code> commands:</p>
<pre><code>plt.plot(voltage365nm,current405nm,"r--",marker="s",label="$\lambda$=365 nm")
</code></pre>
<p>should be </p>
<pre><code>plt.plot(voltage365nm, current365nm,"r--",marker="s",label="$\lambda$=365 nm")
</code></pre>
