<p>The docs are <em><a href="https://docs.python.org/3/library/stdtypes.html?highlight=setdefault#dict.setdefault" rel="nofollow">here</a></em>. </p>
<p><code>dict.setdefault()</code> is something like the "lvalue" analog of <code>dict.get()</code>, which is typically an "rvalue" (occurs on the righthand side of assignments â€” it retrieves a value, as opposed to mutating the state of something).</p>
<p>Informally, <code>d.setvalue(key, value)</code> means "Give me <code>d[key]</code>. What, <code>key</code> isn't in <code>d</code>? OK, then assign <code>d[key] = value</code>, just in time; <em>now</em> give me <code>d[key]</code>."</p>
<p>Suppose <code>d = {'a': 17}</code>. Then</p>
<pre><code>&gt;&gt;&gt; d.setdefault('a', 0)
17
</code></pre>
<p>Here, <code>'a'</code> is in <code>d</code>, so <code>d.setdefault('a', 0)</code> leaves <code>d</code> unchanged and returns <code>d['a']</code>. However,</p>
<pre><code>&gt;&gt;&gt; d.setdefault('b', 100)
100
</code></pre>
<p>Because <code>b</code> is not in <code>d</code>, <code>d.setdefault('b', 100)</code> returns 100 <em>and</em> sets <code>d[b] = 100</code>. <code>d</code> now has two items, and both <code>a</code> and <code>b</code> are keys:</p>
<pre><code>&gt;&gt;&gt; len(d), d['a'] == 17, d['b'] == 100
(2, True, True)
</code></pre>
